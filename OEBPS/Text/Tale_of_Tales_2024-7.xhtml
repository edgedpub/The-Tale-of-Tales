In the midst of this, a certain cricket-leg slave girl, who often went to the fountain to draw water with a barrel, and who knew about the epitaph, for it was spoken of everywhere, having seen Zoza pouring out so much weeping that it flowed in two rivulets, stood by, until the amphora was well done, to take the work out of her hand and leave her with a fistful of fistfuls. And now that he saw her asleep, he drew the amphora rightly from her lap, and, bending over her eyes, in four squeezes filled it to overflowing. No sooner had it been filled than the prince, as if waking from a great sleep, rose from that white marble chest and gave a hand to that mass of black flesh. And, immediately drawing her to his palace, with festivities and royaln fireworks, he made her his wife.

Zoza woke up, and finding the pitcher on the ground, and with the pitcher her hopes, and seeing the grave open, her heart closed in such a way that she was on the verge of unpacking the bundles of her soul at Death’s customs. But finally, since there was no remedy for the evil, and she could complain of nothing but her own eyes that had not looked well at the calf of her hopes, she set off at a slow pace into the city. Where, having heard of the prince’s feast and the fine quality of wife she had taken, she no doubt imagined how the deed had passed, and said, sighing, that two black things had placed her on the bare earth, sleep and a slave. Nevertheless, in order to try everything possible against death, from which every animal defends itself as much as it can, she rented a beautiful house in front of the prince’s palace, from where, if she could not see the idol of her heart, she could at least contemplate the walls of the temple in which the good she desired was enclosed. But one day, having noticed her, Tadeo, who, like a bat, was always flying around that black night of the slave girl, became an eagle to look always fixedly into the person of Zoza, who was the excess of nature’s privileges and the “I call myself out” of the terms of beauty.

The slave girl became aware of this, and, pregnant as she was, threatened her husband with the words: “If window don’t rise, me punch belly and little Giorgie kill!”. Tadeo, tender of his offspring, trembling like a reed for fear of giving her any disgust, tore himself, like a soul from his body, from Zoza’s sight.
