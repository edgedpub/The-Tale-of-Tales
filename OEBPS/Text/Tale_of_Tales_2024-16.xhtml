Open napkin! And the napkin was opened, and immediately upon it were seen, in a crowd, objects of luxury, gallantry, preciousness, beautiful and wondrous things. And then Antuono pronounced: Tighten up, napkin!, and, locking everything up, he set off for the same tavern as last time. When he arrived there, he said to the innkeeper: ‘Take this napkin, keep it for me, and take care that you don’t say: “Open up and take your napkin!”’ He, who was a cunning man, replied: ‘Leave it to me’; and having given him plenty to eat, and having made him grab his little boy by the tail, he sent him to sleep. Then he took the napkin, pronounced the words, and saw before him so many precious things as to amaze. So he found another napkin like it, and replaced it with a brush.

Antuono, waking up and riding at a good trot, arrived at his mother’s house, and shouted: ‘Now we will kick beggary in the face! Now we will put away rags, rags and tatters!” And, laying the napkin on the ground, he said, “Open, napkin!” But he could have said that from today until tomorrow, which was a waste of time, and that did not give the slightest sign of opening. Then, seeing that the matter was going backwards, he said to his mother: ‘Heaven be blessed! The innkeeper has done it to me again. But go, for he and I are two. Better that he had not been born! Better that he had fallen under the wheels of a cart! May I lose the best piece of furniture in my house, if, when I pass by that tavern, to pay for my stolen jewels and donkey, I don’t shatter his vases, bowls, and glasses!” But his mother, who heard this new asinery, spitting fire, rebuked him: “Break your neck, excommunicated son! Break the chain of your shoulders! Stand before me! For I see my bowels and cannot digest you, and my hernia swells and I get a goitre whenever you come between my feet!

End it soon, and may this house burn you like fire! I say I’ll shake my cloths, and count me never having shat you in the world’.

The wretched Antuonus, who saw the lightning, did not want to wait for the thunder; and, like one who has stolen a laundry, lowering his head and raising his heels, he fled towards the ogre.
