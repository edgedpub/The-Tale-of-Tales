When he reached Marigliano, before setting foot in his house, he began to shout, as if scalded by nettles: ‘Run, mother, run; for we are rich! Spread towels, spread sheets, spread blankets, for you will see treasures!”. The mother, with great glee, opened a chest, where she kept her daughters’ trousseau, took from it fine sheets that would fly if you blew, tablecloths smelling of laundry, blankets of colours that would jump in your face, and made a beautiful spread. And Antuono led his donkey onto it and began to chant: Arri, cacauro! But, for Arri cacauro he said, the donkey made as much reckoning of those words as he did of the sound of the lira. But, repeating them three or four times, and all thrown to the wind, he took up a big cudgel and started beating the unfortunate beast; and he clubbed and clubbed so much that the poor animal felt its body melt and made a nice yellow splash on those white rags.

The wretched Masella, who saw this success, and, when she was making a foundation of enriching her poverty, found herself faced with a very different foundation, so liberal as to make her whole house a misery, grabbed a wood, and, giving Antuono no time to show her the pumice stones, gave him a good thrashing. And that immediately sprang at the ogre.

The ogre saw him approaching more at a trot than at a pace, and, since he knew what had happened to him as a fairy, he rebuked him for having allowed himself to be mocked by an innkeeper, calling him a fool, “mamma mia, put me in your mouth”, a sucker, a loon, a simpleton, a lout, a lout and a fool, who, in exchange for a donkey laden with treasure, had been given a beast fertile with ordinary excrement. Antuono, swallowing this pill, swore that never, never again would he allow himself to be tricked and mocked by a living person.

But, after a year, the same ache in his head was renewed, languishing for the desire to see his parents again. The ogre, who was ugly in the face and beautiful in the heart, gave him leave this time as well, and made him a present of a beautiful napkin, saying: “Take this to your mother; but beware, don’t lead yourself to the donkey, as you did with the donkey; and until you get home, don’t say Open or Close, napkin, for if any other misfortune happens to you, the damage is yours. Go now with the good year, and return soon.
