At the same point, Zoza began to ruminate and chew the old woman’s words, the tempting spirit entered her head, and, turning a wheel of thought and a mill of doubt around this fact, in the end, drawn by the chariot of that passion which blinds the judgment and enchants the reasoning of man, having taken a handful of shields from her father’s caskets, she too went out of the royal palace. And so far she went that she came to the castle of a fairy, and having vented her heart to her, the fairy, out of compassion for such a beautiful young girl, who was driven into a precipice by her youth and the overbearing love of an unknown thing, gave her a letter of recommendation for a sister of hers, also a fairy. The latter received her with many compliments, and the next day, at dawn’s rising, when Night makes the birds throw down their banners, promising a good tip to the one who brings her news of a flock of lost black shadows, she gave her a beautiful walnut, saying: “Take it, my child, and hold it dear, and do not open it except in time of great need”.

At which, having arrived after a long journey and received the same kindnesses, she had yet another letter for a fourth sister, and a chestnut, and the same warning that had been given her for the walnut. She walks on and arrives at the castle of the last fairy, who caresses her a thousand times, and in the morning, as she leaves, gives her a hazelnut, with the same protest not to open it if necessity did not kill her.

Having received these things, Zoza set out on the road between her legs, and through so many towns she rode, so many woods and streams she passed, that after seven years - at the very moment when the Sun had saddled his horse to run the usual post, awakened by the crows of the roosters, - she arrived almost at Round Field. Here, before entering the town, she saw the marble sepulchre, at the foot of a fountain, which, because it was enclosed in a porphyry prison, wept crystal tears.

And she took out the pitcher, which she found hanging there, and taking it between her legs, she began to play the comedy of The Doubles, she below and the fountain above, never taking her head from the mouth of the amphora; so that in less than two days she had reached two fingers upon her neck, and not two more were missing, and she would have been full. But, before she had accomplished this last fill, tired from so much weeping, she was, without being able to resist, deceived by sleep, and forced to retire for a couple of hours under the curtain of her eyelids.
