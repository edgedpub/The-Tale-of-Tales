The Tale of Tales

It is a well-established proverb, of those of ancient coinage, that he who seeks what he must not, finds what he does not want; and it is known that the monkey, in putting on his boots, was caught by the foot. And so it happened to a ragged slave girl, who, never having worn shoes on her feet, wanted to put the crown on her head. But since the grindstone smoothes out all the roughness, and there comes a day when all is redeemed, she who had usurped what belonged to others by the wrong means, finally stumbled upon the wheel of kicks and the higher she climbed, the greater the tumble; as is narrated in this book.

Once upon a time there was a king of Hairy Valley, who had a daughter called Zoza, who, like a second Zoroaster or Heraclitus, had never been seen to laugh. The wretched father, who had no other spirit than this one daughter, did nothing to take away her melancholy, and made those who walked on clubs and those who jumped through hoops come up to tease her into laughing, now the acrobats, now master Ruggiero, now the jugglers, now the forces of Hercules, now the dog dancing, now the jumping monkey, now the donkey drinking from the glass, now bitchy Lucia, and now this and now that. But it was time wasted, for not even the rhyme-god of Master Grillo, not even the sardonic herb, not even a blow in the diaphragm would have wrinkled the faintest smile in her mouth. The poor father, not knowing what else to try, ordered a large fountain of oil to be opened in front of the palace door as a final test, with the thought that the people, who passed like ants in the street, would jump like crickets, leap like goats and run like hares, slipping and bumping into each other when the oil splashed, so as not to get their clothes greasy, and in this way some case would arise that would excite his daughter to a burst of laughter.

When this fountain had been opened, and Zoza was standing at the window, so well composed that she looked like vinegar, an old woman came by chance, soaking up the oil with a sponge and squeezing it into one of her jars.
