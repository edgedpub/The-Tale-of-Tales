Antuono, taking the donkey, without saying good evening, got on it and set off at a trot. But he hadn’t taken a hundred steps when, dismounting from the donkey, he shouted: Arri, cacamo! And he had scarcely opened his mouth when that Sardinian began to pour out pearls, rubies, emeralds, sapphires and diamonds, each as big as a walnut. Antuono, with an open palm of his throat, looked at those beautiful outpourings of body, those superb discharges, those rich dysentery of the donkey; and, with great jubilation, filling a saddlebag with those joys, he climbed back in and, tapping his feet with good energy, reached the tavern. When he had dismounted, the first thing he said to the innkeeper was: ‘Tie this donkey to the manger; feed it abundantly; but mind, don’t say: Arri, cacamo!, for you would regret it. And keep these little things in a safe place”. The innkeeper, who was of the four of art and a master of mischief, hearing this unexpected warning, and seeing the jewels worth thousands, was curious to know what effect those words had. And so, having prepared Antuono a good meal and given him as much to drink as he could, he had him shoved between a big bag and a slave girl; and as soon as he saw his eyelids droop and his snoring was at full blast, he ran to the stable and said to the donkey: Arri, cacamo! And the donkey, with the medicine of these words, performed the usual operation, giving the body a diarrhoea of gold and turbid gems.

On seeing this precious evacuation, the innkeeper drew up a plan to swap the donkey and bungle that tawdry man Antuono, considering it easy to blind, bind, deceive, trick, sack and show blisters for lanterns to a brown, sheepish, simple-minded, brown, maccarone pig, as he had come into his hands. When Antuono woke up in the morning, when Aurora went out to throw the old man’s pot, full of red sandstone, at the eastern window, he rubbed his eyes with his hands, stretched his arms for half an hour, yawned and farted about sixty times in the form of a dialogue, and called the innkeeper, saying:

“Come here, comrade: thick bills and long friendship; friends who are in war with each other: give me the bill and pay me. And so, so much for bread, so much for wine, this for soup, that for meat, five for stabling, ten for bed, and fifteen for gratuity, he paid out the money; and, taking the forger’s donkey with a bag of pumice stones in exchange for the ringstones, he set off at a good pace for his country.
