Antuono, who, when he least expected it, saw himself being groomed, combed and lined, as soon as he could slip out of her hands, turned on his heels. And so much did she walk that, at about twenty-four hours, when the lanterns were beginning to light in Cinzia’s shops, she arrived at the foot of a mountain so high that it clashed with the clouds.

There, on the root of a poplar tree, near a cave carved out of pumice stone, sat an ogre: oh mamma mia, how ugly he was! He was dwarfed and bodacious, his head was bigger than a guinea-pumpkin, his forehead was bumps, his eyebrows were conjoined, his eyes were distorted, his nose was flattened, his nostrils looked like two master’s nostrils, his mouth was the size of a millstone, and he had two tusks that reached his ankles; his chest was hairy, his arms like a reel, his legs bent as if he were a vault, and his feet were as wide as a duck’s. In short, he looked like the devil. In short, he looked like a devil, a parasak, an ugly beggar, and a bad shadow, who would have stunned an Orlando, terrified a Snivelling Beak, and made the most skilful fencer swoon.

But Antuono, who did not move with a sling, bowed his head and said to him: ‘Goodbye, sir, what’s up? How are you? Do you want anything? How far is it from here to where I must go?”. The ogre, who heard this discourse from pole to pole, laughed, and, as he liked the beast’s humour, said to him, “Do you want to stay as master?” Antuono answered: “How much do you want per month?”. And the ogre said: “Wait to serve me honourably, that we may agree and you will have a good life”.

And so, when this relationship was over, Antuono remained in the ogre’s service, at whose house food was thrown in his face and, as far as working was concerned, he was an armchair; so that in four days he became as fat as a Turk, as round as an ox, as bold as a cockerel, as red as a prawn, as green as garlic and as big as a whale, and so thick-skinned and taut that he could hardly open his eyes.

It had not been two years, when, having been bothered by so much grascia, he had a great desire and longing to take a ride to Marigliano; and, thinking of his little house, he was consumed and had almost returned to his former appearance.
