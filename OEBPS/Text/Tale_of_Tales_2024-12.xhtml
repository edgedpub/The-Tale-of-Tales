He who said that fortune is blind, proved wiser than Master Lanza (may he pierce him!), for truly it gives blind blows, raising to the top people whom you would not chase out of a field of broad beans and throwing down others, who are the flower of men, as I will tell you by an example.

It is said that once upon a time, in the town of Marigliano, there was a well-to-do female called Masella, who, besides six unmarried daughters, similar to six poles, had a son, so slob-like, so beastly, that he was not fit for the game of snow4; so much so that she stood like a sow with the bar in her mouth, and not a day went by that she did not say to him: ‘What are you doing in this house, you damned bread? Beat it, you piece of rascal! Clear out, maccabeo! Sink, you plunderer! Get in front of me, you nut-eater! You were changed for me in the cradle, and in the place of a little child, of a pacioncello, of a handsome jockey, there was placed before me a pappalasagne pig!” With all this, Masella spoke and he whistled.

Seeing that there was no hope of Antuono (that was his son’s name) turning his head to do right, one day, having washed his gourd well without soap, he gave his hand to a rolling pin and began to take the measure of his jacket.

IThe Tale of the Ogre

First Entertainment fo the First Day

After Antuono of Marigliano is kicked out by his mother for being the ringleader of all oafs, he enters into the service of an ogre, from whom he receives a gift whenever he wants to go back and see his home. Antuono is tricked each time by an innkeeper, but finally the ogre gives him a club that punishes him for his ignorance, makes the innkeeper do penance for his ruses, and brings Antuono’s family riches.
