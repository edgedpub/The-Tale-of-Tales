The unfortunate Antuono tucked his tail between his legs and sipped this music; and he spent more than three years quietly at the ogre’s service, thinking of his house as much as of becoming a count. But after that time, the desire to make a trip to his house came back to him, and he asked the ogre for permission again. And the ogre, pressed by his insistence, was content to let him go, and gave him a finely crafted mace, with the warning: “Take this mace with you for my sake, but take care not to say: Get up, mace, or lie down, mace, for I want no part of you. Antuono, receiving her, answered: “Go, now I have put on the tooth of wisdom and I know how many pairs of oxen three pairs can make: I am no longer a boy, and whoever wants to cheat Antuono wants to kiss his elbow”. Replied the ogre: ‘The work praises the master: the words are female, the deeds are male: we shall see! You have heard me more than a deaf man: man forewarned, half saved’.

The ogre continued talking, and already Antuono was hurrying home. But it was not half a mile away that he said: Get up, mace! It wasn’t a word, it was the art of enchantment: the mace, immediately, as if it had been rammed into the marrow, began to work on the miserable Antuono’s shoulders; and the blows rained down, and one blow didn’t wait for the other. The poor man, who saw himself beaten and bruised like the skin of a Cordovan, cried out: Lie down, mace!’, and the mace ceased making counterpoints on the stave of his back. So, instructed at his own cost, he said: ‘Cripple him who flees! Affé, that this time I will not let her escape! Not yet has he gone to bed who must see the evil evening!”.

With these thoughts he arrived at the usual tavern, and was received there with the greatest welcome in the world, because the innkeeper knew what sauce was made from that rind. Antuono said to him: ‘Take, keep this mace for me; but take care not to say: Get up, mace, that you may be in danger. The innkeeper, who was overjoyed at this third venture, plied him well with soup and showed him the bottom of the pitcher; and when he had put him, drooping with sleep, into a small bed, he ran to fetch the mace, and calling his wife to attend the beautiful feast, he said: Get up, mace!
